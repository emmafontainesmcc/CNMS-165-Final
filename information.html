Mustang Management in the United States

The management of Mustangs within the United States is largely done in public areas of Western states. According to the U.S. Bureau of Land Management, there are tens of thousands of wild Mustangs, though their populations have exceeded the capacity of the land, thus necessitating management efforts such as roundups and relocation programs. This indicates that Mustangs are not "extinct" in the sense of being eradicated altogether but are threatened due to lack of habitat space and human versus land competition.

Wild Mustangs are under pressure because of their habitats, population controls, and isolated horse communities. Mustangs breed naturally without many predators, causing populations to increase rapidly. There are times when governments remove Mustangs from the wild for overgrazing.

Mustangs are managed today by adoption programs, fertility control, and protected status. Many removed horses are placed into homes through adoption systems. Over the decades, hundreds of thousands of horses have been adopted.

How can you help?

  • Support ethical adoption programs. Adopting a mustang (when properly trained and cared for) helps reduce pressure on holding facilities and gives horses homes.
  • Support habitat conservation. Organizations working to protect public rangelands help ensure horses have space to live naturally.
  • Volunteer. Some programs allow volunteering with adoption events or educational outreach connected to wild horse care and placement programs.
